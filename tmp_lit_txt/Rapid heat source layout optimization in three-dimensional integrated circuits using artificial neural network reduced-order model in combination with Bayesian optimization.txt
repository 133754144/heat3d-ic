            1    |    INTRODUCTION
            Three‐dimensional integrated circuits (3D ICs) incorporate a significant array of through
            silicon vias (TSVs) and hierarchical packaging architectures, culminating in highly integrated
            3D ICs configurations. Such configurations introduce intrinsic thermal management
            challenges.1–3 Addressing thermal management issues pertinent to 3D ICs necessitates the
            optimization of TSVs geometric parameters and strategic placement of heat sources. Given that
            one end of 3D ICs is typically dedicated to electrical interconnections, the opposing end
            becomes the sole avenue for heat dissipation, thereby constraining the dissipation pathways.
            Heat conduction via TSVs emerges as a pivotal heat transfer conduit, underscoring the
            importance of TSVs optimization and methodical heat source placement. Strategically
            positioning heat sources within 3D ICs, coupled with the enhanced heat dissipation afforded
            by the superior thermal conductivity of TSVs, is theoretically significant for thermal regulation
            in these circuits. In practice, the optimization challenges evolve into complex, high‐
            dimensional, nonlinear, and multiextremal problems that are computationally intensive and
            resource‐demanding. Constructing a predictive model from pre‐existing training data and
            utilizing this model for optimization can substantially decrease the execution time of
            optimization algorithms. Conventional machine learning algorithms encompass decision trees,
            random forests, support vector machines, and neural networks.4 The neural network was used
            for training and validation of the amassed data, with the application of neural networks as
                                                                                            4,5
            reduced‐order models being well‐established in thermal analysis.                    Artificial neural networks
            (ANN) obviate the necessity of formulating mathematical equations between inputs and
            outputs, operating as black‐box models that generate corresponding mathematical representa-
            tions through the training, testing, and validation of input data.6–8 Feedforward neural
            networks, a specific type of neural network model, can establish corresponding mathematical
            models with a limited number of sample points, fit the nonlinear relationship between inputs
            and outputs, and swiftly obtain the required objective function values. Employing feedforward
            neural network‐based reduced‐order models can efficaciously mitigate the computational
            resource expenditure inherent in numerical simulations.
                In the thermal analysis of 3D ICs, the optimization of material property parameters and the
            distribution of heat sources presents a significant computational challenge. Moreover, robust data
            support is essential for the thermal management optimization of TSVs. The complexity of factors
            influencing heat source distribution and the extensive solution space render the development of an

            2.3     |   Structuring of neural network prediction model
            In this study, we explore the impact of positional variations of four heat sources on the
            temperature field of a chip, with each source confined to a specified area. Achieving
            comprehensive coverage of sample points for this analysis necessitates significant computa-
            tional resources. The complexity is further compounded when the number of heat sources is
            increased, leading to an exponential rise in the required number of samples. Given the
            computational demands associated with finite element simulations, it is imperative to
            minimize the simulation workload. The construction of a neural network predictive model
            presents an efficient alternative. By employing a neural network to approximate the simulation
            model, it is feasible to develop a surrogate model with high accuracy using a relatively small
            data set, thereby substantially reducing the need for extensive data sampling.
                Keyparameters that define the architecture of a feedforward neural network model include
            the size of the sample data set, the number of hidden layers and neurons, the choice of transfer
            function, and the training function. The conventional architecture of a feedforward ANN
            reduced‐order model is depicted in Figure 3, providing a visual representation of the model's

            (2) Sampling scheme for data
            This data‐set was produced through a sampling process that utilizes the COMSOL application
            programming interface (API) with MATLAB. A three‐layer stacked 3D IC model, constructed
            within the COMSOL Multiphysics 5.5 software, was interfaced with MATLAB. Various heat
            source positions were then selected within MATLAB for batch sample generation, while each
            sample was simulated once in COMSOL. To minimize the sample size without compromising
                                                                                                                    21–23 This
            accuracy, the data collection process incorporated Latin hypercube sampling (LHS).
            method merges the benefits of random and stratified sampling. Initially, the sample space is
            uniformly divided into N segments, followed by the selection of a random value within each
            segment to represent the sampling point for that interval. This approach ensures
            comprehensive coverage of the sample space while maintaining the randomness of sampling.
                Given that LHS is a form of stratified sampling, the extracted sample points are evenly
            distributed across the entire sample space. This method divides the range of each variable into
            uniform subintervals, each with an equal probability of being sampled. Random selection
            within these intervals ensures that the sample points are both comprehensive and
            representative, effectively minimizing the risk of selecting extreme cases. Each representative
            sample is likely to be found within a designated subinterval, and the random sampling of each
            subinterval substantially increases the likelihood of selecting any specific representative
            sample. Consequently, LHS is highly effective in capturing all representative samples within
            the sample space, thus enhancing the representativeness and reliability of the sampling process.
            To illustrate this methodology, Figure 4 demonstrates a typical two‐dimensional implementa-
            tion of LHS. In such a setup, each distinct element appears exactly once in any given row or
            column, ensuring that each variable's full range is sampled without repetition. This
            arrangement facilitates a thorough and unbiased exploration of the multidimensional sample
            space.
                Although the partitioning method for sample point selection reduces the total number of
            points, it fails to encompass all potential heat source locations for the optimization function.
            Conversely, the finite element simulation approach, which involves numbering unit blocks,
            aligns the heat source with the finite element unit model blocks, thereby facilitating batch data
            generation. To address the issues arising from partition sampling, each unit block's position
            was divided into a combination of central coordinates and heat source dimensions. This

                        conjugate gradient method. To determine the most suitable training function for this model,

                        from the optimal value. As the iteration progresses, the rate of gradient descent significantly

                        algorithm's primary strength lies in its rapid convergence speed and broad applicability.

                        substantial increase in computation time when dealing with numerous input parameters. The

                        method. This makes it a primary method for solving nonlinear optimization. However, it does
                        have a notable drawback: while its solution method is relatively accurate and fast for quadratic

                This method calculates the posterior distribution of f(x) from the current observation data
            and determines the next search point using the acquisition function based on Gaussian
            distribution information. The Bayesian part of the optimization comes from the use of Bayes'
            theorem to update the probabilistic model based on new data. This allows the method to
            incorporate all the information that has been gathered during the optimization process, making
            it a very efficient method for optimizing expensive functions. The BO process involves the
            following steps:
            (1) Define the required f(x) and the domain of x.
            (2) Take a finite number of n values of x and calculate f(x).
            (3) Estimate the objective function based on the finite observation values and obtain the target
                 value on the estimated function f*.
            (4) Define the rules and determine the next observation point.
                Repeat steps (2)–(4) until a predefined stopping criterion is met, such as reaching a
            maximumnumberofiterations or exhausting computational resources. The fundamental
            concept underlying BO involves guiding the search process through the creation of
            a probabilistic model of the objective function. This model aids in identifying the
            parameter configuration that allows the objective function to attain a globally optimal
            solution.

            4    |    CONCLUSIONS
            In this study, we have employed a methodology that combines ANN reduced‐order model
            with BO to predict and optimize the temperature distribution in 3D ICs with variable heat
            source layouts. We have developed a three‐layer stacked 3D ICs model, which allows for the
            parametric positioning of heat sources. The use of LHS has been instrumental in reducing
            the computational data requirements for this study. The COMSOL Multiphysics API,
            interfaced with MATLAB, facilitated the batch generation of the necessary training data. A
            mathematical model was then formulated to predict the effects of heat source placement on
            the temperature distribution within the 3D ICs. The trained reduced‐order model
            underwent global optimization via BO to ascertain the optimal parameters for heat source
            positioning. The predictive results were subsequently cross‐verified with actual finite
            element simulation outcomes to confirm the precision of the prediction model and the
            efficacy of the optimization algorithm. For the 3D ICs model constructed in this study, BO
            identified the global optimum within a remarkably short time frame of 4.07s over 250
            iterations. In comparison, the surrogateopt algorithm required 64s and 470 iterations to
            complete the optimization process. The GA, while faster than surrogateopt at 5.83s,
            demanded a considerably higher number of iterations, totaling 2500, to pinpoint the

                        opposed to BO's single evaluation, which underscores BO's lower computational resource

                        determinestheoptimaldistributionofheatsourcepositionsin3DICs,theoptimizationof
                        the reduced‐order model still requires the generation of a substantial amount of
                        simulation data. Future research could focus on devising methods for constructing

                        is limited to examining the effects of heat source positioning on the overall temperature
                        distribution of 3D ICs. Subsequent research could expand the scope to include the impact
                        of varying material property parameters, thereby advancing thermal management
